NYT: Nikon Hits the Sweet Spot With New D5000:

If youâ€™re considering an entry-level Nikon D.S.L.R., you might want to aim higher. The company has just announced the D5000, a model that bridges the gap between its popular D60 and D90 models by offering features like high-definition video capture, a larger sensor, an 11-point auto-focus, and Live View at a reasonable price of $849. Nikon D5000

Ahem. Bridging an approximate gap of $500, landing on the higher end of that gap, of course. And that’s 720p HD, presumably the same performance as the D90, which means don’t even think about video unless you’re on a tripod using a VR (vibration-reduction) lens.

The D5000 is built upon the small chassis of the D60 (which costs approximately $600) but adds many of the finer attributes of the D90 (now selling for roughly $1,200, with lens). The 12.3-megapixel D5000 employs the same sensor as the D90, for instance, and it offers the same 11-point auto-focus system (the D60 has only three AF points). That means the D5000 will provide faster and more accurate auto-focus than the D60.

Ugh. What the hell are you thinking, Nikon? This tiny gap did not need to be filled by a camera that takes the biggest drawback of the D60, namely it’s chassis (which lacks an auto-focus motor for non-AF-S lenses, or lenses with built-in auto-focus motors). The video is nice, but the D90 body is about the same price.

The D5000 also packs a whopping 19 automatic scene modes (as compared with 5 on the D90) as well as several new in-camera editing features, including a soft filter effect to smooth out faces and a perspective control that enables users to correct distortions of up to 10 percent that often appear in architectural photographs.

“Whopping”!? Are you retarded? I wonder what icon they’ll use for “Indoor Stadium - Picture of the Game” mode vs. “Retractable Roof Stadium, Closed - Picture of the Game.” No one needs 19 automatic modes. The owner will need the manual to figure out which mode is “right”. But you already know everyone will just take the fully-automatic mode 95% of the time. Not to mention the fact that the group you’re marketing this to, the advanced amateur, (or people with money to burn?) should be using an aperture- or shutter-priority mode when shooting 100% of the time.

Argh. Nikon, you needed to close the $1,500 gap between the D90 and the D700 with a full-HD camera that provides video output equivalent to the Canon 5D mark II. And preferably, keeping it a cropped sensor so you still have that strong delineation between cropped sensors and full-frame sensors. Better low-light performance for still photography and video would be a bonus, but I won’t push my luck.
